You Know What You Need To Do, But Can't Do It

Understanding the ADHD Communication Gap: It's Not About Knowing the Rules—It's About Being Unable to Execute Them in the Moment

People with ADHD are often told they're rude, inattentive, lazy, or don't care—when the truth is the brain knows exactly what to do but struggles to do it during real-time conversation. This isn't a character flaw. It's a neurocognitive performance deficit. Let's explore what's really happening.

The Performance vs. Knowledge Gap

What You Know

  • Don't interrupt
  • Remember what they said
  • Wait your turn
  • Control your reactions

What Your Brain Juggles In Real-Time

  • Listening actively
  • Holding thread in working memory
  • Formulating response
  • Inhibiting interruption
  • Reading facial cues
  • Managing emotions

"Social interaction is an executive function marathon—and your brain is running with weighted ankles."

Why Conversations Feel Impossible

Losing the Thread (Working Memory)

+

People with ADHD can lose the conversation thread mid-sentence, ask questions that were already answered, or talk continuously to 'dump' thoughts before they evaporate.

No Filter (Impulsivity)

+

Thoughts become words instantly—no buffer between brain and mouth. They interrupt not from rudeness but because if they wait, they might forget.

Intense Emotional Reactions

+

Conversations trigger flooding. A small criticism feels like a tidal wave, leading to withdrawal or heavy emotions.

The Relationship Cost

"He says he loves me, but he forgets to pay the mortgage"

Affection doesn't compensate for executive dysfunction in relationships. This often leads to a "demand-withdraw cycle" or a "parent-child dynamic" where one partner can feel overburdened.

Stat: Inattention is the most corrosive symptom of a long-term relationship. The partner can feel alone and uncared for.

What Actually Helps

Keep this hopeful and practical.

  • DBT skills (STOP): Stop, Take a deep breath and step back, Observe, Proceed mindfully.
  • Structured couple therapy: repeat back exactly what the other person said. Start with "What I hear you saying is..."".
  • Workplace accommodations: Written instructions, quiet spaces, meeting recordings.

"You don't need to be 'fixed'—you need tools that work WITH your brain, not against it"

My Personal Experience

I was diagnosed with ADHD as an adult, and when I discovered this, so many things made sense. I've struggled to maintain quality relationships, or be a good friend. Outside looking in can look like I don't care about my relationships, but I really do. For a long time, I thought I was just broken, and I sometimes got frustrated that I don't have a "normal" brain. But my message is that none of us are broken. And I don't think any of us have a "normal" brain. Everyone has challenges, but that doesn't make us broken. Because of these challenges, we can come together and have compassion for eachother. This changes everything.
